Our Star to Steer By

Salesmanship,
Multiplied.

A good agency has a point of view — a star to steer by. Ours is simple: the purpose of advertising is to sell. Not to win awards. Not to entertain. Not to "keep the brand out there." To sell — measurably, provably, profitably.

Claude Hopkins called advertising "multiplied salesmanship" — one salesperson presenting to thousands of prospects at once. The principles that make for outstanding success in personal selling work just as well in marketing, whether the medium is an envelope, a Meta feed, a TikTok video, or an AI-generated answer. Here are the five we build every engagement on.

1

Belief comes first.

Salespeople sell more when they're true believers — and so do marketers. We only take on products and services we'd proudly recommend to our own family and friends. When the answer is an unqualified yes, the work is a labour of love, and it shows in the results.

2

Knowledge does the selling.

Before we write a word or spend a dollar, we dig: your product, your market, your offer history, your back end, what a customer is truly worth to you. We gather far more material than we'll ever use, so only the strongest selling points survive. Benefits are the building blocks of persuasion — and you can't build with blocks you never found.

3

The presentation must be powerful.

With one message reaching thousands at once, the presentation has to be brilliant. Ours are built on the Persuasion Equation below — and they're never afraid of long copy. The more you tell, the more you sell.

4

The numbers keep everyone honest.

In direct response, complete and accurate records are the navigation charts to fortune. Every campaign we run reports its cost per lead, cost per order, and return on spend — which means you can measure us, dollar for dollar. We welcome that. It's the whole point of being accountable.

5

We play to win.

We don't bunt. In every campaign we swing for the result that doubles or triples your sales and slashes your cost per order — because the surest way to build our reputation is to make each carefully selected client a model of success.

The Persuasion Equation

What Your Advertising
Should Say

"What you say is more important than how you say it." Before any question of channel, creative, or budget, every campaign we build must answer the universal customer question — "What's in it for me?" The strategy we use to answer it has been working since selling began:

Problem + Promise + Proof + Proposition = Persuasion

The Problem

Zero in on the most important problem your product solves for your prospect — ideally one your competitors' advertising ignores. Markets are customer problems, not demographics.

The Promise

Aim a big, specific promise squarely at that problem. "Promise, large promise, is the soul of an advertisement." Vague claims don't sell; specific ones do.

The Proof

Build a case strong enough to persuade a jury — testimonials, case histories, guarantees, credentials, demonstrations. Weak proof is the single greatest cause of advertising failure.

The Proposition

Close with an offer that makes it easy — and urgent — to say yes: risk reversal, guarantees, premiums worth having, deadlines that mean something.

The next time you want to judge an ad — ours included — ask four questions. Does it key into the right problem? Does it make a strong, clear-cut promise? Does it prove its case, or merely make claims? Does it close with a proposition that makes it easy to act now? You'll be astonished at the holes these questions reveal in most campaigns.
